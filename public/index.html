Hiring & Contracts
Rules governing employment contracts, probation periods, minimum ages, and digital Qiwa requirements. Two significant changes in the February 2025 amendments affect every employer in KSA.
★ Updated Feb 2025Royal Decree M/44Arts. 37–81Applies to: All employers

The KSA Labour Law provides for two main contract types:

  • Unlimited (indefinite) contracts — available to Saudi nationals only. No fixed end date.
  • Fixed-term contracts — mandatory for all non-Saudi (expatriate) employees. Typically one year, renewable.

Part-time contracts also exist. Part-time is defined as working fewer than half the usual daily hours at the establishment. There are no specific contract types for non-profit organisations.

Labour Law Art. 37 · Unchanged in Feb 2025
February 2025 change: The Qiwa electronic contract is now the sole legally recognised form. Paper contracts have no legal effect. All contracts must be executed and signed via the MHRSD Qiwa portal. Previously, courts sometimes treated paper and Qiwa contracts differently — this ambiguity is now resolved.

The Qiwa contract must contain:

  1. Name and address of the employer
  2. Name and nationality of the employee
  3. Identity proof (e.g. Iqama or passport number)
  4. Place of residence
  5. Salary
  6. Start date of employment
  7. Duration (for fixed-term contracts)

Companies may still issue an internal company contract alongside the Qiwa contract, but in any dispute the Qiwa contract is definitive. In the event of a discrepancy, whichever term is more favourable to the employee will apply.

Labour Law Art. 51 as amended by Royal Decree M/44 · Effective Feb 2025
February 2025 change: The probation period can now be agreed upfront at up to 180 calendar days (previously: 90 days, with a separate written agreement needed to extend by another 90 days). Both parties now explicitly have the right to terminate during probation. The probation period can also be waived entirely by mutual agreement.

Key rules:

  • Must be stated in the employment contract to be valid
  • Either party may terminate during probation without compensation
  • Implementing Regulations specify which leave types are excluded from the probation count
  • An employee may only serve one probation period per employer
Labour Law as amended by Royal Decree M/44 · Effective Feb 2025
February 2025 change — Default term for non-Saudis: If a fixed-term contract for a non-KSA national does not specify a duration, it automatically defaults to one year from the actual start date and renews automatically for the same duration unless either party gives notice. Previously, it defaulted to the work permit expiry date.

For Saudi nationals: if a fixed-term contract is renewed three times, or the total employment period under fixed-term contracts reaches four years, the employment relationship automatically becomes unlimited.

For non-Saudi nationals: the contract never converts to unlimited. It renews for the same fixed term unless notice is given by either party.

Labour Law Art. 37 as amended by Royal Decree M/44 · Effective Feb 2025

Yes. All Qiwa contracts must be in writing and signed electronically through the Qiwa portal. There is no longer a valid paper-based alternative.

Every employee must have a Qiwa contract in place — and a valid work permit — before commencing work. The work permit is obtained via the MHRSD and requires a Qiwa contract to be in place first.

Labour Law Arts. 37, 51 · Feb 2025 amendment

The minimum age for employment is 15 years. In limited cases, the Minister may allow juveniles aged 13–15 to undertake light work, provided it does not harm their health, morals, or interrupt school attendance.

Restrictions for juvenile employees (15–18 years):

  • Cannot work in harmful industries or professions that endanger health, safety, or morals
  • Cannot work during the night; must have at least 12 hours of rest
  • Maximum 6 hours per day (4 hours during Ramadan)
  • Break of at least 30 minutes after every 4 hours worked
  • Cannot work on weekends, public holidays, or annual leave days
  • Employer must obtain guardian approval and a health and fitness certificate
Labour Law Arts. 75–81 · Unchanged in Feb 2025

There is no express statutory requirement in the KSA Labour Law to conduct criminal record checks. However, it is common practice in sectors working with children or vulnerable people (e.g. education, healthcare).

Employers may request references from former employers — yes, this is permitted. There are no employee representative bodies or collective bargaining agreements in KSA.

Pay & Compensation
Wages, overtime, housing allowances, and end-of-service gratuity. Three of the four articles in this section were updated by the February 2025 amendments, including a brand-new obligation on housing and transport.
★ Updated Feb 2025Royal Decree M/44Arts. 61–107Applies to: All employees

There is no statutory minimum wage in the KSA for private sector employees. Salaries are agreed contractually between employer and employee.

Wages are due on dates agreed between the parties — typically monthly. Wage transfers are monitored via the Wages Protection System (WPS), which verifies that salaries are paid in full and on time.

February 2025 change — Payslip transparency: Employers must now issue itemised monthly payslips showing basic salary, all allowances, and any deductions. The WPS payment window is strictly 30 days; delays may result in suspension of Ministry services for the employer.
Labour Law Art. 90 as amended · Effective Feb 2025
February 2025 change — New statutory obligation (Article 61): Employers must now either (a) provide suitable housing and transportation directly, or (b) pay a housing allowance and a transportation allowance in lieu. This is a brand-new obligation not previously addressed in the Labour Law. If no specific amount is stated in the contract, it is determined by prevailing practice and mutual agreement.

Key implications for employers:

  • Must be specified in the Qiwa employment contract
  • Housing and transport allowances are now included in the remuneration base for end-of-service gratuity calculations
  • Particularly impactful for companies employing large numbers of expatriates
Labour Law Art. 61 as amended by Royal Decree M/44 · Effective Feb 2025

Where an employee works more than their contractually agreed working hours, they are entitled to overtime compensation:

  • Rate: Normal hourly wage plus a 50% uplift calculated on the basic wage
  • Public holidays: Employees required to work on a public holiday must also receive overtime compensation at the same rate

Exempt categories (not entitled to standard overtime):

  • Senior management with the same authority as the employer over workers
  • Preparatory or complementary work done before or after main working hours
  • Work that is inherently intermittent
  • Guards and cleaning workers (excluding civil security guards)
February 2025 change — Compensatory leave option: Employers may now offer paid compensatory time off instead of cash for overtime (1.5 hours leave per overtime hour worked), with the employee's written agreement. Any unused compensatory leave must be paid out in cash upon the employee's separation.
Labour Law Art. 107 as amended · Effective Feb 2025

End-of-service gratuity (EOSG) is a lump-sum payment due to all employees (Saudi and non-Saudi) upon termination of employment. It is calculated as follows:

Years of serviceGratuity per year
First 5 years15 calendar days’ basic salary per year
After 5 years30 calendar days’ basic salary per year

No qualifying service period is required — even employees with less than one year of service receive a pro-rated gratuity. Incomplete years are calculated on a pro-rated basis.

February 2025 change — Housing in gratuity base: Housing and transport allowances must now be included in the remuneration base used to calculate end-of-service gratuity. Previously, only basic salary was the standard base in many contracts.

Gratuity reduction on voluntary resignation:

  • Less than 2 years’ service: no gratuity
  • 2–5 years: one-third of full entitlement
  • 5–10 years: two-thirds of full entitlement
  • 10+ years: full entitlement (no reduction)

Employees receive their full usual salary during annual leave. Accrued but untaken annual leave must be compensated in cash upon termination.

Labour Law Art. 109 · Unchanged in Feb 2025

Employees are entitled to a total of 120 calendar days of sick leave per year:

  • First 30 days: full pay
  • Next 60 days: 75% pay
  • Final 30 days: no pay

A medical certificate is required. There is no additional entitlement to unpaid sick leave beyond the 30 days above.

Labour Law Art. 117 · Unchanged in Feb 2025
February 2025 change: Maternity leave increased from 10 weeks to 12 weeks on full pay. At least 6 weeks must be taken immediately after childbirth. The remaining 6 weeks may be taken flexibly, starting no earlier than 4 weeks before the expected due date.
  • 12 weeks on full pay (up from 10 weeks)
  • Minimum 6 weeks must be taken after childbirth
  • Up to 4 weeks may be taken before the expected due date
  • An additional 1 month unpaid may follow
  • If the child has special needs requiring constant care: additional 1 month on full pay, extendable by 1 month unpaid
Labour Law Art. 151 as amended by Royal Decree M/44 · Effective Feb 2025

Employees are entitled to 3 days of paid paternity leave.

February 2025 change: The leave must now be taken within 7 days following the child's birth. Previously there was no time limit, creating uncertainty for both parties. There is no unpaid paternity leave entitlement.
Labour Law Art. 113 as amended by Royal Decree M/44 · Effective Feb 2025
February 2025 change — New sibling entitlement: 3 days of paid leave are now granted upon the death of a sibling. Previously, bereavement leave only covered spouses, ascendants, and descendants.

Full bereavement entitlements:

  • Death of spouse, ascendants, or descendants: 3 days paid
  • Death of a sibling: 3 days paid (NEW — Feb 2025)
  • Female Muslim employees upon death of spouse: Iddah leave — 4 months and 10 days paid
  • Non-Muslim female employees upon death of spouse: 15 days paid
Labour Law Art. 113 as amended by Royal Decree M/44 · Effective Feb 2025

Employees are entitled to paid time off on national holidays. Fixed public holidays:

  • Eid Al-Fitr: 4 days
  • Eid Al-Adha: 4 days
  • Saudi National Day: 1 day (23 September)

Exact dates for Eid depend on moon-sighting and are announced annually by authorities. Employees required to work on a public holiday must receive overtime compensation (normal wage + 50% uplift on basic).

Labour Law Art. 112 · Unchanged in Feb 2025
  • Hajj leave: 10–15 days on full pay, granted once during the entire employment period (not once per employer). Includes Eid Al-Adha holidays.
  • Marriage leave: 5 days paid upon the employee’s own marriage.
  • Study leave: Duration and pay as agreed between employer and employee. Certain conditions and documentation requirements apply.

Employers cannot require employees to use annual leave in place of a statutory special leave entitlement.

Termination
Resignation rules, employer termination grounds, final settlement payments, and dispute resolution. The February 2025 amendments formalised the resignation process for the first time and added new grounds for termination.
★ Updated Feb 2025Royal Decree M/44Arts. 74–87Applies to: Employees
February 2025 change — Structured resignation process introduced: The amendments define resignation for the first time in the law and establish a clear process for fixed-term contracts.
  • Resignation must be submitted in writing
  • The employer has 30 days to respond
  • If no response is given within 30 days → resignation is automatically accepted
  • The employer may delay acceptance for up to 60 days for operational reasons, with written justification provided before the initial 30-day period expires
  • The employee may withdraw their resignation within 7 days unless it has already been accepted

Employees can resign for any reason by adhering to contractual terms. There is no stipulated list of grounds required.

Labour Law as amended by Royal Decree M/44 · Effective Feb 2025

A fixed-term contract can only be terminated before its expiry date for gross misconduct as defined under Article 80 of the Labour Law. Examples include:

  • False identity or documents submitted by the employee
  • Causing substantial financial loss to the employer
  • Being under the influence of alcohol or drugs at work
  • Physical assault on the employer or colleagues
  • Disclosure of confidential business information
  • Unauthorised absence for 30+ days in a year (or 15 consecutive days)

Where termination is not for grounds listed in Article 80, the terminating party must pay compensation. This can be up to the salary for the remaining period of the fixed-term contract, subject to a minimum of two months’ remuneration.

Labour Law Art. 80 · Unchanged in Feb 2025

Notice under an unlimited term contract can be given at any time for a valid reason — typically poor performance or misconduct.

Where an unlimited contract is terminated without a valid reason, compensation is payable: 15 days’ pay per year of service, subject to a minimum of two months’ remuneration.

February 2025 change — New termination grounds: Employer bankruptcy and force majeure are now explicitly recognised as valid grounds for termination. Unlawful dismissal is subject to stricter, enforceable financial penalties under the updated law.
Labour Law Arts. 74–80 as amended · Effective Feb 2025

On termination, employees are generally entitled to:

  1. Salary during the notice period — minimum 60 calendar days for unlimited contracts
  2. Compensation for accrued but untaken annual leave
  3. End-of-service gratuity — 15 days’ basic salary per year for the first 5 years; 30 days’ per year thereafter. No qualifying service period required.
  4. Compensation for wrongful or early termination (where applicable) — minimum two months’ remuneration
  5. Repatriation ticket to the employee’s home country (or agreed destination) unless the employee obtains alternative sponsorship
February 2025 change: Housing and transport allowances are now included in the remuneration base for end-of-service gratuity calculations.
Labour Law Arts. 84–87 as amended · Effective Feb 2025

Before filing a claim with the Labour Court, employees must first go through a mandatory MHRSD mediation process. If mediation fails, the matter is referred to the Labour Court.

February 2025 change — Written grievance process: When an employer imposes a disciplinary penalty, written notice must now be provided to the employee. The employee may challenge the penalty by submitting a written grievance within 30 days (excluding official holidays). Digital mediation via the Qiwa platform is the primary channel for dispute escalation.

There is no principle of vicarious liability under the KSA Labour Law. Termination must always be documented with a written notice to the employee.

Worker Types
Employees, independent contractors, volunteers, and non-citizens — how they are defined under KSA law and what rights each category holds. The 2025 amendments strengthened the equal treatment mandate for all workers regardless of nationality.

Under the KSA Labour Law, an employee is defined as every physical person, male or female, working for an employer and under the management or supervision of the latter for a wage, even if away from direct surveillance.

An independent contractor or consultant is not defined in the Labour Law. In practice, a contractor is typically a licensed company providing services to another company. Individuals working directly for a company are almost always classified as employees and require a work permit and employer sponsorship.

Key distinctions:

  • Contractors invoice fees (not a salary)
  • Contractors are not entitled to Labour Law benefits from the end-user
  • Contractors must hold their own commercial licence to operate in KSA
  • Contractors are not sponsored by the end-user for residence visa or work permit

Consultancy agreements are governed by contract law, not the Labour Law. Termination grounds and liability are entirely determined by the consultancy contract.

Labour Law Art. 1 (definitions) · Unchanged in Feb 2025

Contractors receive no statutory benefits from the end-user engager:

No annual leaveNo sick leaveNo maternity leaveNo paternity leaveNo overtime payNo health insuranceNo social securityNo workplace injury coverNo unemployment benefitsNo EOSGNo housing allowance

There is no minimum pay requirement for contractors. Consultancy agreements should be drafted on a case-by-case basis; no prescribed template exists. Written contracts are strongly recommended as courts rely primarily on written evidence in disputes.

KSA Labour Law · Commercial law · General contractual principles

There is no definition of “volunteer” in the KSA Labour Law. A volunteer is understood as someone who works for a charitable cause without receiving remuneration. Volunteers are not considered employees and most employment regulations do not apply.

  • No written agreement is required (though recommended)
  • Organisations may pay stipends and reimburse expenses (transport, food, etc.)
  • No income or withholding taxes apply to volunteer stipends in KSA
  • No working hour restrictions, leave entitlements, or social security obligations
  • Liability for volunteer-caused damages depends on the contractual arrangements between the organisation, the volunteer, and any end beneficiary
KSA Labour Law · No specific article for volunteers · Common interpretation

Non-citizen employees are covered by the KSA Labour Law in almost the same way as Saudi nationals. The key differences are:

  • Contract type: Non-Saudi nationals may only hold fixed-term contracts. Unlimited contracts are available to Saudi nationals only.
  • State pension (GOSI): Only Saudi nationals (and GCC nationals under a GCC-wide arrangement) participate in the state pension scheme. Non-GCC expatriates receive end-of-service gratuity only.
  • Work authorisation: Every non-citizen requires a KSA residence visa (Iqama) and a work permit. Employers are responsible for obtaining work permits for their employees.
February 2025 change — Equal treatment mandate: The law now explicitly prohibits discrimination against any employee regardless of nationality, based on race, colour, gender, age, disability, or marital status. Non-citizens are entitled to equal protection of employment laws in any dispute.

Asylum-seekers and forcibly displaced persons without a valid residence visa cannot legally work in KSA, regardless of their protection status.

Compliance
Qiwa contract obligations, Saudization enforcement, and the equal opportunity mandate. All three articles in this section were updated by the February 2025 amendments — compliance is now more strictly monitored through digital enforcement.
★ Updated Feb 2025Royal Decree M/44Arts. 35–43Applies to: All employers
February 2025 change — Qiwa is now the only valid contract form: All employment contracts must be documented and signed electronically via the MHRSD Qiwa platform. Paper contracts have no legal effect. Courts will only recognise Qiwa-registered contracts in disputes.

What must be on Qiwa:

  • All new employment contracts
  • Remote and hybrid work arrangements (new obligation — Feb 2025)
  • Contract renewals and amendments

Penalties for non-compliance:

  • Ministry of Human Resources and Social Development can withhold work permit renewals
  • Fines of SAR 200,000–500,000 for entities providing manpower services without a proper licence
Labour Law as amended by Royal Decree M/44 · Effective Feb 2025

Saudization (Nitaqat) refers to the government policy requiring private sector companies to employ a minimum percentage of Saudi nationals, varying by sector and company size. A number of professions are reserved exclusively for Saudi nationals.

February 2025 change — Enhanced enforcement: The MHRSD now uses digital AI audits that cross-reference employment records, visa data, and payroll information. Non-compliance consequences have been strengthened: the Ministry may now deny work permit renewals for breaches of any labour regulations, not just Saudization requirements. Employees affected by non-compliant employers can transfer to another employer without the current employer’s consent.

Mandatory training obligation (new in 2025): Employers with 50 or more employees must establish training and qualification programmes focused on Saudi nationals. Non-compliance may result in work permit suspensions.

Labour Law Arts. 35, 42–43 as amended · Effective Feb 2025
February 2025 change — Explicit legal duty: The 2025 amendments add a specific obligation on employers to ensure equal opportunities and prohibit discrimination based on race, colour, gender, age, disability, marital status, or any other characteristic. This applies across all employment decisions: hiring, promotion, compensation, training, and dismissal.

What employers should do:

  • Update HR policies, recruitment criteria, and job descriptions to reflect equal opportunity principles
  • Put in place mechanisms for receiving and investigating harassment complaints (Anti-Harassment Law)
  • Ensure the workforce is aware of these mechanisms
  • Review disciplinary procedures for consistency

Non-discrimination and anti-harassment policies are strongly recommended even where not explicitly mandated by law.
